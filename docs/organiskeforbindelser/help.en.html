We use different names for differently sized carbon-chains. The ending -ane, -ene, -yne or -yl tells us what type of hydrocarbon it is. Cyclic compounds get cyclo- as prefix.

2. Functional groups

A functional group is what gives functionality to the compound. The more functionality it gives, the higher it is prioritized in naming. A compound with both a hydroxy (OH) and carboxy (COOH) group will for example be called a hydroxy acid because the physical and chemical properties the acid group provides are more important for the molecule as a whole than the alcohol group. The functional group of the highest priority determines both the compound's last name (suffix) and the direction of counting.

3. Numbering the carbon chain

Number the parent chain such that the functional group of the highest priority gets the lowest possible number. This means we count from the end the functional group is closest to. All other groups are considered substituents and will give rise to the beginning of the compound's name.

4. Multiple substituents

When you have multiple of the same kind of group, you write down the position for all of them and separate the numbers by commas. You must also specify before the name of the substituent how many you have by using the greek words: di-, tri-, tetra-, etc.

5. Alphabetical order

When you have multiple different kinds of substituents, you write them in alphabetical order. Only consider the name of the substituent when ordering, do not use the greek word in front. When comparing "2,4-dimethyl" and "3-ethyl" for example, you compare "methyl" to "ethyl".

6. Esters (COOR)

Esters have their own nomenclature rule. They are made from a condensation reaction between an alcohol and a carboxylic acid. The alcohol's name comes first and gets the ending -yl. The carboxylic acid's name comes after with the ending -oate. This is because deprotonated acids have the ending -ate, such as nitrate, sulfate and carbonate.

7. Ethers (R-O-R)

Ethers have their own nomenclature rule. In the past you used to name them by mentioning the length of the two carbon chains attached to the oxygen and put ether at the end. For example: ethylmethylether. Now we instead consider the longest carbon chain the main molecule and the smaller chain the substituent. Since there is an oxygen between the smaller chain and larger chain it is not an alkyl attached to the larger chain, it is an alkoxy. If you have C-C-O-C-C-C we would call it ethoxypropane for example.
